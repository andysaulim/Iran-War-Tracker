What We Know — Confirmed & Estimated Totals
No day-by-day breakdown exists publicly. This timeline shows only data points that have been confirmed by the Pentagon or formally estimated by CSIS. The bar in each row represents the share of the D+12 estimate.
Mar 5, 2026 H+100
CSIS estimates first 100 hours cost $3.7B
$3.7B
CSIS published its first estimate using CBO cost data for participating units, plus munitions replenishment and attrition estimates. The implied daily rate is $891.4M/day. Of the $3.7B, approximately $3.5B is unbudgeted — $196M in operations and support is mostly budgeted. CSIS noted at publication that "the cost will be highly sensitive to the actual number and mix of munitions expended" and that it awaited further details from DOD.
CSIS Estimate
Mar 10, 2026 D+2 partial
Reuters: $5.6B in munitions costs through D+2
$5.6B
Reuters reported that the Trump administration estimated munitions expenditures in the first few days cost $5.6 billion. This figure covers munitions replacement costs only — it is not a total war cost figure and is not comparable to the CSIS H+100 or Pentagon D+6 totals. CSIS notes the Pentagon's $11.3B is "broadly consistent" with this figure.
Munitions Only · Not Total Cost
Mar 11, 2026 D+6
Pentagon reports $11.3B cumulative to Congress
$11.3B
The NYT reported that DOD informed Congress the first six days cost $11.3B. This exceeds CSIS's H+100 estimate, confirming heavier-than-expected munitions use. The figure explicitly excludes: (1) pre-war military buildup; (2) facility repair costs; (3) some equipment replacement costs. The White House had not publicly confirmed or cleared the figure. OMB methodology may differ.
Pentagon Confirmed
Mar 13, 2026 D+12
CSIS revises estimate: $16.5B through Day 12
$16.5B
Using the Pentagon's $11.3B as an anchor, CSIS revised its methodology and estimated the campaign had cost $16.5B through Day 12. CSIS also revised the combat losses figure upward from $359M to ~$1.4B, incorporating the confirmed KC-135 loss, 11 MQ-9 Reapers, and a THAAD AN/TPY-2 radar, alongside additional base damage visible in overhead photography. Daily costs had "declined substantially" since the opening days.

Why D+6 exceeds H+100 The $11.3B confirmed at D+6 significantly exceeds CSIS's $3.7B H+100 estimate. CSIS attributes this to heavier munitions use than initially modeled. Munitions — Inventory & Strategic Risk
The munitions transition — Gen. Caine, D+4 (Mar 7) On D+4, General Caine reported U.S. forces had reached a "point of munitions transition" — shifting from standoff cruise missiles fired outside enemy range to "stand-in precision strikes overhead Iran." A Tomahawk costs $3.6M; a JDAM kit costs $80,000 — a 45× difference. This transition, enabled by degraded Iranian air defenses, is the primary driver of declining daily costs. B-1 and B-52 bombers began CONUS-based operations over Iran with heavier bomb loads. LUCAS drones and PrSMs were used in combat for the first time.

How DOD Might Pay for This
The following is drawn directly from CSIS analysis. All characterizations are CSIS's own words.
CSIS — Mar 5 & Mar 13, 2026 "The DOD will need additional funds at some point because the level of budget cuts needed to fund this conflict internally would likely be politically and operationally difficult." CSIS identifies three possible mechanisms, noting that "any funding action will become a focal point for opposition to the war."
MechanismCSIS assessment
Supplemental appropriation
Standalone bill to cover war costs
 "The Trump administration might decide to ask for a supplemental appropriation to cover the war and any other unexpected expenses across the government. The Bush administration did that at the beginning of the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan."
FY2027 reconciliation bill
Fold costs into future legislation
 "The Trump administration might ask for the funds in a FY 2027 reconciliation bill, although it is not clear whether there will be such a bill."
Divert existing reconciliation funds
Redirect DOD's $150B from first reconciliation bill
 "The administration could ask Congress to divert some of the DOD's $150 billion from the first reconciliation bill to cover these expenses." CSIS notes this "would undermine the concept behind the increase: to build capability, not pay existing bills."
